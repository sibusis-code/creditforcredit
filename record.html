Your credit record

Academic credit becomes financial credit. Do well, earn credits, and build one record that pays you back — in a qualification you keep, and in what you can prove to anyone who asks.

What's on it

Everything you've earned, in one place

Every module you complete goes onto your Credit Record: what you studied, the credits it carried, when you finished it, and how well you did. Not a folder of certificates — one running record of the work.

Because delivery runs through an accredited provider, what sits on the record is verified rather than self-reported. That is the difference between saying you can do something and being able to show it.

The record belongs to you. Not to the employer who paid for the module, and not to us. You change jobs; it goes with you.

It holds two kinds of thing, and it keeps them apart honestly: credit-bearing modules, which carry credits toward the qualification, and professional development such as the workplace wellness courses, which earn a certificate of completion. Both are worth having. Only the first one ladders.

How it builds

Do the work, and it counts

Four steps, repeated. Nothing you finish is ever wasted.

1 · Learn

Pick a module that matters for the work you're doing or the work you want. Days, not months.

2 · Earn credits

Finish it properly and you earn credits toward the Occupational Certificate: Computer Technician — real credits, not points.

3 · Bank it

The credits land on your record and stay there, with how well you did alongside them. Doing well is the point.

4 · Prove it

A verified record you can put in front of an employer or a college — and the thing you apply with when you claim a credit refund.

Yours to share

You control who sees it

A record is only worth something if you can show it to people — and only safe if nobody can show it without you. So sharing is something you do, deliberately, one party at a time.

You grant access. You can withdraw it. Nothing is visible to an employer, a recruiter or an institution unless you have said yes to that specific party.

We don't sell your data. Not to recruiters, not to advertisers, not to anyone. If somebody wants to see what you've achieved, they ask you — and you answer.

Guidance, not instructions

A record that can point at what's next

Because your record knows what you've done and how you did it, it can be useful about what comes next — the module that follows on naturally, the gap between where you are and a qualification, the thing worth doing before the thing you're aiming at.

That's a suggestion, and it stays a suggestion. Nothing here decides your career for you or scores you against anyone else. You know things about your life that a system never will. The record's job is to make sure the work you've already done is visible when you make the call.
